Help

Gemma currently has the ability to perform only one kind of meta-analysis, for recurring patterns of co-expression. Additional capabilities will be added in the future.

Caveats

  • Not every data set is available for every type of analysis. In some cases data sets are not sufficiently annotated yet, or the data set is too small to use. However we provisionally retain the data.
  • Results may vary over time, as we reannotate the sequences on the microarrays, add data sets, or modify algorithms.

Recurring co-expression analysis

The methods used for "recurring coexpression" analysis are essentially as described in Lee et al, 2004. Briefly, each data set is preprocessed and analyzed to identify pairs of genes that are strongly coexpressed. The on-line analysis searches these results for pairs of genes that are coexpressed in multiple data sets, starting from a gene you choose.

For example, starting with YFG, you might discover that YFG is coexpressed with YOFG in 4 independent studies. The value '4' is the stringency at which the pair of genes YFG + YOFG are coexpressed. By changing the stringency to higher or lower values, you can reduce or increase the number of results you get back. You can also limit the expression data sets that are included in the search.

Interpretation

The idea behind recurring co-expression analysis is simply an extension of the idea of using coexpression on a single data set: that two genes that are coexpressed are more likely to be functionally related that two genes that are not coexpressed. Thus we would like to be able to say that genes that are coexpressed in multiple data sets are even more likely to be functionally related. Not too surprisingly, we found this wass the case in our previous work.

There are two types of false positives you may get. First, statistical false positives are genes that are observed as coexpressed in multiple data sets purely by chance. Second, there can be "biological false positives", in which two genes might be coexpressed but inferring a strong functional relationship between the genes would be misleading or incorrect.

Choosing the correct stringency is important, as results that are observed (say) only twice in 50 data sets might not be due to chance. While we are still calibrating the analyses, our prior work showed that in a specific database of 60 data sets, a stringency of 3 or 4 was sufficient to weed out most false positives.

The second type of false positives are harder to address. Our earlier studies showed that in terms of Gene Ontology categories, each data set a pair of genes was coexpressed in was "worth", on average, an additional Gene Ontology term in common. That is, genes coexpressed in 5 data sets would on average share 2 more GO terms than genes coexpressed in 3 data sets. The catch is this is "on average" and also surely depends on the data sets used. Therefore, we primarly offer coexpression results as a form of hypothesis generation that benefits greatly from addition of other prior information. Improving this aspect of the analysis is the topic of ongoing research.
